Interview

1. What development tools have you used?

So far, I’ve mainly used Notepad++ for writing HTML, CSS, and JavaScript in my Web Page Development class. I’ve also used Visual Studio Code a little bit when I wanted something with more features. Before going back to school, I didn’t have much experience with actual development tools. Most of my work has been with industrial software like Rockwell and ABB programming tools, along with Microsoft Office. I’m still learning the web development side, but I’m getting more comfortable with it.

2. What programming languages have you used?

Right now I’m learning HTML, CSS, and JavaScript through my current class at NSU. I don’t have a lot of traditional programming experience yet. In my industrial work I’ve worked with ladder logic and different PLC programming languages, but that’s more control systems than regular coding. I plan to keep learning more languages as I go through the CIS program.

3. What technical certifications do you hold?

I don’t currently hold any professional IT or cybersecurity certifications. I do have my technical diplomas in Industrial Electronics and Industrial Instrumentation from Louisiana Technical College, and I completed advanced electronics training in the Navy. I’m planning to start working on CompTIA certifications soon.

4. How do you maintain your technical certifications?

Since I don’t have any active IT certifications yet, I haven’t had to worry about maintaining them. Once I start earning them, I plan to stay current by taking the required continuing education courses and keeping up with new technology through my job and schoolwork. I’ve always tried to stay on top of new equipment and systems at work, so I think that habit will help me stay current with certifications too.

5. How is your education helping prepare you for the job market?

Going back to school has been really helpful. My classes are teaching me things like web development, networking basics, and cybersecurity concepts that I didn’t have before. The cybersecurity research project I did also gave me a better understanding of real-world threats and how organizations need to protect their systems. I think having both my hands-on industrial background and this new education is going to help me stand out for roles that involve OT systems and security.

6. How would you rate your key competencies for this position?

I would rate my troubleshooting and problem-solving skills very high because that’s what I’ve done in my career for over 25 years. I’m also strong with industrial control systems, reading schematics, and working with different types of equipment. On the IT side, I’m still building my skills, especially with programming and networking. I think I bring a good mix of real-world experience and a willingness to keep learning.

7. What are your IT strengths and weaknesses?

My biggest strength is being able to troubleshoot complex systems and figure out what’s wrong, even when it involves multiple types of equipment. I’m also pretty good at learning new technology when I have to. My main weakness right now is that I’m still new to traditional IT and programming. I don’t have years of experience with things like databases, scripting, or network administration yet, but I’m working on that through school and plan to keep improving.

8. Describe a recent project and your responsibilities.

One project I’m proud of is the cybersecurity research project I did for the NSU and NRMC grant. I did most of the research and wrote the full paper on cybersecurity threats facing medical facilities. I also handled a lot of the coordination for the group and helped put together the presentation. It was a lot of work, but it helped me learn how to research properly and present information clearly. It also gave me a better understanding of how important cybersecurity is in real organizations.

9. What challenges might you expect in this position?

I think one of the biggest challenges would be balancing the technical side with the security side, especially when it comes to older industrial systems. A lot of OT equipment wasn’t originally designed with cybersecurity in mind, so finding ways to secure those systems without breaking operations can be tricky. I also know I’ll need to keep learning because technology changes fast. I’m ready for that, but I know it will take consistent effort.

10. Why is it important to work directly with business users?

I think it’s important because people actually using the systems every day usually know where the real problems are. They can tell you what’s working and what’s not in a way that technical documentation can’t. If you don’t talk to them, you might build or secure something that looks good on paper but doesn’t actually help the people who need it. Working with business users helps make sure the solutions actually solve real problems and get used the right way.
